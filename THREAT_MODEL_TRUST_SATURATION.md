Supplementary Threat Intelligence Report: Resource Saturation Attacks in Authentication Processes and the Collapse of the Monetary Value of ‘Trust’
1. Overview: Zero-Day Attacks on the Social OS
The essence of this protocol (commonly known as the Tagosaku Protocol) lies not merely in the forgery of digital data or localised hacking. It is a structural DDoS attack designed to invalidate the very monetary value of ‘trust’—the foundational OS of modern society—and to deliberately deplete the physical and human resources of the defencers.

2. Definition of Vulnerability: A Desperate Asymmetry of Costs
While existing security models specialise in protecting ‘systems and networks’, final identity verification (eKYC, job interviews, administrative application procedures) still relies on ‘human eyes and time’.
By utilising generative AI, the attacker can generate an infinite supply of extremely sophisticated forged information (fictitious CVs, ID documents, portfolios) at ‘zero cost and within seconds’. In contrast, the defender must expend ‘finite human resources’ to verify its authenticity. This overwhelming disparity in the exchange ratio constitutes a fatal vulnerability that cannot be remedied by existing patches.

3. The Ripple Effect of Attacks and the Collapse of ‘Trust’
If attackers utilise anonymous apps or social media (crowds of unspecified individuals) as proxies to flood the system with forged entries via this protocol, the following incidents will occur in a chain reaction.

Complete Freeze of Front-End Systems:
Tens of thousands of sophisticated forged data entries will flood the recruitment departments of private companies and government agencies. The labour costs and time required to verify a single entry multiply, bringing legitimate business processes to a complete standstill.

Deflation of social ‘trust’:
As it becomes virtually impossible to verify the authenticity of online data, the ‘verification’ process itself becomes uneconomical. Consequently, society is forced to regress to inefficient ‘face-to-face’ interactions or closed networks reliant solely on kinship and personal introductions.

The Collapse and Mutation of Criminal Organisations:
If this method is applied to the recruitment processes of criminal organisations that rely on anonymity (such as black-market part-time jobs), it can induce mutual distrust within the organisation, enabling it to self-destruct or collapse at no cost. However, this is a double-edged sword; should this attack method become widespread, the targets will extend to all manner of organisations.

4. The Absence of an ‘Antidote’
Within the current architecture, there is no complete antidote (systemic defence measure) against this attack. Even if AI-based counterfeit detection filters are introduced, it amounts to nothing more than a game of cat and mouse, with the attackers exploiting the specifications of the detection algorithms to optimise their methods and slip through the filters; the underlying structure, which continuously erodes the operational costs of the defenders, remains unchanged.

5. Proposed Defence Strategy: The Need for Specialised, Isolated Monitoring Nodes
Since the system cannot prevent this automatically, the only countermeasure is to rebuild a ‘monitoring network that detects signs of anomalies (noise) at the front line and continuously flags logical inconsistencies’.
In this monitoring and auditing work, the outdated practice of commuting to a physical office and the traditional organisational structure serve only as an attack surface that breeds peer pressure and human error.
What is truly required is a ‘specialised audit unit’ capable of navigating logs and websites using only cold, hard logic, whilst operating in an environment completely isolated from external noise (fully remote and non-face-to-face), to extract minute errors and shortcomings. The presence of monitors who fundamentally understand the destructive toxicity of this protocol is the minimum prerequisite for guaranteeing the security of the next era.
